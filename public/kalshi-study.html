Case Study

An 8-week, pre-registered empirical study putting Kalshi's central marketing claims to the test — with 1,000 retail sportsbook bettors and their own trading history as the control.

The claim we're testing

“On Kalshi there is no house,
anybody can win.”

Through aggressive marketing, Kalshi has worked to convince the average retail bettor that they're trading against their peers and have a real chance to succeed on the platform. The primary goal of this research is to highlight the claims Kalshi has made publicly and test them with the average retail bettor.

The foundational principles around prediction markets, is like a lot of these markets, the people that will price them the best may not actually be the experts or the authority figures that you usually would think about.

— Tarek Mansour, Kalshi CEO · Cheeky Pint podcast, Mar 2026

Users who place resting orders on Kalshi (which include a wide variety of users, not just institutions) compete with each other based on their assessments of the underlying event's probability. Market forces drive the ‘price’ toward a fair value, unlike a sportsbook, where the house can charge any amount it wishes to its individual customers.

— Kalshi, to Sportico · Sept 2025

Who you're really trading against

“There is no house.”

Kalshi positions itself as a peer-to-peer exchange where retail users trade against each other. In practice, institutional market makers post the deepest, tightest quotes — and for RFQ-priced parlays, only API-onboarded firms can quote at all.

The order book

Anyone can technically be a Maker, but institutional firms end up on the Maker side of most retail trades. Academic data on Kalshi's own tape finds Takers lose ~32% while Makers lose ~10% — a 22-point edge at the venue level.

The RFQ parlay system

Retail can't post resting orders on parlays. They request a quote, institutional makers price the other side via API, and retail gets a take-it-or-leave-it price. Every parlay fill is, by design, against an institutional maker.

What it does — and doesn't — confirm

What this confirms

  • The cohort is the exact population Kalshi markets to and acquires — young, male, employed, weekly-active retail bettors with no Wall Street background. The textbook “average Joe.”
  • The Branch 2 benchmark is cleaner than expected — Hard Rock dominance means most users are benchmarked against the same book.
  • The ≤5 prior-trade criterion is realistic — 65% have zero prior prediction-market experience.

What it doesn't confirm

  • Geographically concentrated in Florida — may not generalize across state regulatory environments.
  • 87% male — inference limited to male retail bettors.
  • 92% under 35 — inference limited to younger bettors.
  • Skews working-to-middle class — may not generalize to high-net-worth bettors or the >$500K-volume cohort Citizens JMP identified as the only profitable Kalshi tier.
How the study runs

What we measure

Forecast accuracy, not just P&L.

The headline statistics are strictly proper scoring rules that converge faster than dollar returns — so luck washes out and skill shows through over a realistic window.

Primary outcomes

  • Brier score — per-trade forecast calibration (lower is better)
  • Log loss — per-trade forecast accuracy (lower is better)
  • Closing-line value — entry price vs. vig-removed closing price at the user's own book or Kalshi mid

Secondary & descriptive

  • Net P&L per user — gross and net of fees
  • Maker vs. Taker fill share by user and contract type
  • Implied maker margin on RFQ parlay quotes
  • RFQ refusal rate — requests that produced no fillable quote

Scientific structure

The full study design.

The complete experimental structure — variables, controls, hypotheses, and operational definitions. It's dense by design; tap any section to expand.

IVIndependent variables — what we vary
  1. Venue. Sportsbook (pre-period) vs. Kalshi (treatment period). The primary manipulation.
  2. Contract type on Kalshi. Single-event vs. RFQ-priced parlay. Affects which counterparty class the cohort faces and what mechanism is in play.
  3. Counterparty class. Maker-initiated fill vs. Taker-initiated fill (institutional MM proxy vs. retail proxy).
  4. Position size. Bet/order size in dollars. Used to test whether maker margin scales with size.
  5. Pre-period skill tier. Cohort stratified into quartiles by pre-period CLV against their own sportsbook. Used to test whether Kalshi affects skilled vs. unskilled users differently.
DVDependent variables — what we measure
Primary
  • Per-trade Brier score (forecast calibration; lower is better)
  • Per-trade log loss (forecast accuracy; lower is better)
  • Closing-line value (CLV) — the gap between entry price and the vig-removed closing price at the user's own book or Kalshi mid
Secondary
  • Net P&L per user, gross and net of fees
  • Maker-vs-Taker fill share by user and by contract type
  • Implied maker margin on RFQ quotes (cohort's quoted price vs. theoretical fair value)
  • RFQ refusal rate (share of cohort requests that did not produce a fillable quote)
Descriptive
  • Per-user trade volume and frequency
  • Bet size distribution
  • Sport mix
  • Time-to-quote and time-to-stale on RFQs
CVControl variables — what we hold constant or stratify on
  • User identity. Each user is their own control (within-subject design). Eliminates cross-user confounds in baseline skill, sports knowledge, risk appetite, and capital level.
  • Sportsbook of record. Each user is benchmarked against the closing line at the book they themselves used — no comparing one book's users against another's.
  • Sport mix. Reported by sport. NFL, CFB, and NBA dominate per the survey; other sports analyzed separately if sample size allows.
  • Time window. Pre-period and treatment period are contiguous, and CLV is measured at the contract resolution timestamp on both sides.
  • Funder identity. Disclosed prominently; analysis pre-registered before any data is collected.
CFConfounding variables — what could distort results, and our controls
  • Selection bias. Participants may differ from non-participants. Control: founding-member survey baseline; final cohort distributions reported separately.
  • Survivorship bias. Dropouts and blow-ups exit naturally. Control: intent-to-trade denominator; low-engagement and dropout cohorts reported as separate strata.
  • Hawthorne effect. Users behave differently when they know they're observed. Control: cohort existence not publicly marketed; tracking is passive after enrollment.
  • Performance-driven behavior. Payment-tied incentives change trading style. Control: flat retention payment only if triggered; no performance-based payouts.
  • Variance vs. skill. Lucky users distort group stats over short windows. Control: Brier-based primary outcomes; medians as the headline statistic; permutation tests at α = 0.01, Bonferroni-corrected.
  • Contamination. Users splitting time across venues dilute the treatment. Control: ≥80% Kalshi-volume threshold for primary analysis; non-compliant users reported as a separate stratum.
  • Counterparty pricing response. Market makers might price differently if they detect a cohort. Control: staggered onboarding; cohort existence not disclosed during treatment.
HHypotheses — pre-registered, falsifiable
  • H1 — Venue effect on CLV. Cohort median Kalshi CLV is no better than cohort median sportsbook CLV. Null: Kalshi is at least as good as the cohort's existing sportsbook.
  • H2 — Counterparty composition. Cohort institutional MM share exceeds the stated 7%. Null: the “peer-to-peer” claim holds for this cohort.
  • H3 — Retail base-rate replication. Cohort Taker return clusters at or below the academic Taker baseline of −32%. Null: cohort performs at or worse than the academic Taker baseline.
  • H4 — Parlay margin. Cohort parlay implied maker margin exceeds the 15% documented average. Null: parlay margin is at or below the venue average.
  • H5 — Size-dependent pricing. RFQ maker margin scales monotonically with request size. Null: margin is independent of size.

Each is tested independently and publishes in either direction. Bonferroni correction is applied across all five at α = 0.01.

OPOperational definitions — what each term means in data terms
  • CLV = (user's entry implied probability) − (vig-removed closing implied probability), in percentage points. Positive = user beat the line.
  • Brier score = (predicted probability − outcome)², where outcome is 1 if the YES contract resolved YES, else 0. Averaged across the user's trades.
  • Log loss = −[outcome × ln(prediction) + (1 − outcome) × ln(1 − prediction)]. Averaged across the user's trades.
  • Maker-initiated fill = trade where the user posted a resting order subsequently matched against an incoming counterparty (from the Kalshi trade tape's taker_side field).
  • Taker-initiated fill = trade where the user hit an existing resting order (the inverse of the above).
  • RFQ implied margin = (cohort's filled price) − (product of vig-removed single-leg probabilities at quote timestamp), in percentage points.
  • Skill tier = pre-period CLV quartile relative to own-book closing lines, computed at enrollment and locked for the treatment period.
  • Cohort inclusion = a user with ≥90 days pre-period history, ≥50 sportsbook bets pre-period, ≤5 prior prediction-market trades at enrollment, ≥30 Kalshi trades during the 8-week treatment window, and ≥80% of treatment-period sports-trading volume placed on Kalshi.
Definitions

Key terms, defined.

Plain-English definitions of the core concepts behind the study — what they mean and why they matter. Tap any term to expand.

01“Wall Street”

The traditional winners in sports betting over time. In prediction markets, “Wall Street” is the market makers. In sportsbooks, it's the sharp bettors whose action sets the closing line.

02Market makers

The professional liquidity providers on Kalshi. They post prices on both sides of a contract, take the other side of retail trades, and profit from the spread. In a binary prediction market every trade needs a counterparty — most of the time, that counterparty is a market maker.

In sportsbooks, the equivalent role is the sharp bettors and the book's own pricing desk: sharps set the line through their action, and the book adjusts based on the wagers it takes.

03Sample contamination

When cohort behavior changes because users know they're in a study, or when outside variables we didn't intend to measure leak in. The results only mean something if the treatment period reflects “sportsbook bettors moving onto Kalshi” — not bettors performing for an audience, or splitting time between Kalshi and their usual books.

04Survivorship bias

Most retail-trading studies overstate performance because they only count users who are still active at the end. Quitters and blow-ups drop out of the denominator, so the surviving group looks better than the original sample ever did.

05User incentives

If users are paid based on performance, they trade differently than they would naturally — which contaminates the experiment. We're measuring what normal sports bettors actually do on Kalshi, not what they do when they're competing for a prize.

06Causality vs. variance

Over a short window of binary outcomes, lucky users outperform and unlucky users underperform regardless of skill. Separating real differences from noise takes statistical machinery — not just a leaderboard.

07Timeframe sufficiency

Whether the testing window is long enough for forecast accuracy to converge and skill to separate from luck, while still short enough to keep users engaged and reflect how they really behave.

08Cohort representativeness

The cohort isn't “all Americans” or “all retail traders” — it's a specific group of young, male, employed, weekly-active retail sports bettors. Pretending otherwise would overstate what the study proves. That specific group is exactly who Kalshi's marketing targets.

09Skill segmentation

Inside any group of 1,000 sports bettors there's a wide skill range — some sharp, most square, a few chasing variance. Reporting only group averages hides the most interesting question: whether Kalshi helps or hurts users differently at different skill levels.

10Handling outlier performers

In short-window binary trading, a single lucky user can distort the headline number — one person hitting six longshot parlays in a row drags the average up. Good reporting leans on medians and full distributions so a few outliers don't define the result.

The open question — when to run the treatment

Once the baseline is set, the 8-week Kalshi-exclusive treatment can start right away or wait for football season. Same study either way — the timing changes the quality of the data and the size of the audience.

Start now · June–July

  • First-mover. Plants the flag before competitors and rides early momentum.
  • Sports dead zone. The cohort lives on NFL, CFB, and NBA — mostly dark in summer.
  • Thin volume. Hard to clear ≥30 Kalshi trades per user, leaving the primary analysis underpowered.
  • Unrepresentative. Off-season trading isn't how these bettors normally behave — results are easy to dismiss.

Football-aligned · Sep–Nov

  • Densest action. Runs across the peak NFL + CFB stretch — the cohort's real interest and Kalshi's highest-volume markets.
  • Hits thresholds. Natural volume clears the ≥30-trade bar and powers the analysis.
  • Stronger baseline. A longer summer baseline tightens CLV and skill-tier stratification.
  • Later result. Findings publish in November instead of late summer.

The way through: decouple momentum from the treatment window. Go public now and build audience all summer — World Cup man-on-the-street, the survey push, and the community play-along campaign — then run the 8-week treatment during football season so the headline data lands when attention peaks. Results publish roughly 12 weeks after the treatment window opens.
