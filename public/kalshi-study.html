Case Study

An 8-week, pre-registered empirical study putting Kalshi's central marketing claims to the test — with 1,000 retail sportsbook bettors and their own trading history as the control.

The claim we're testing

“On Kalshi there is no house,
anybody can win.”

Prediction markets, by their “foundational principles,” are framed as venues where average people — not experts or authority figures — compete against each other. Through aggressive marketing, Kalshi has worked to convince the average retail bettor that they're trading against their peers and have a real chance to succeed on the platform. The primary goal of this research is to highlight the claims Kalshi has made publicly and test them with the average retail bettor.

The foundational principles around prediction markets, is like a lot of these markets, the people that will price them the best may not actually be the experts or the authority figures that you usually would think about.

— Tarek Mansour, Kalshi CEO · Cheeky Pint podcast, Mar 2026

Users who place resting orders on Kalshi (which include a wide variety of users, not just institutions) compete with each other based on their assessments of the underlying event's probability. Market forces drive the ‘price’ toward a fair value, unlike a sportsbook, where the house can charge any amount it wishes to its individual customers.

— Kalshi, to Sportico · Sept 2025

Who you're really trading against

“There is no house.”

Kalshi positions itself as a peer-to-peer exchange where retail users trade against each other. In practice, institutional market makers post the deepest, tightest quotes — and for RFQ-priced parlays, only API-onboarded firms can quote at all.

The order book

Anyone can technically be a Maker, but institutional firms end up on the Maker side of most retail trades. Academic data on Kalshi's own tape finds Takers lose ~32% while Makers lose ~10% — a 22-point edge at the venue level.

The RFQ parlay system

Retail can't post resting orders on parlays. They request a quote, institutional makers price the other side via API, and retail gets a take-it-or-leave-it price. Every parlay fill is, by design, against an institutional maker.

The test

We know how the cohort performs against the books — their sportsbook history is synced via Fade Sync. Across 8 weeks of Kalshi-exclusive trading, we measure whether retail sports bettors perform better on prediction markets than they do on sportsbooks.

Launch-timing implication

Enrollment and Fade Sync baselining should complete in late summer so the 8-week Kalshi-exclusive treatment runs across September–November — the densest stretch of weekly NFL and CFB action. Launching off-season would suppress natural trade volume and put the ≥30-trades-per-user primary-analysis threshold at risk, biasing engagement downward for reasons that have nothing to do with the platform.

What it does — and doesn't — confirm

What this confirms

  • The cohort is the exact population Kalshi markets to and acquires — young, male, employed, weekly-active retail bettors with no Wall Street background. The textbook “average Joe.”
  • The Branch 2 benchmark is cleaner than expected — Hard Rock dominance means most users are benchmarked against the same book.
  • The ≤5 prior-trade criterion is realistic — 65% have zero prior prediction-market experience.

What it doesn't confirm

  • Geographically concentrated in Florida — may not generalize across state regulatory environments.
  • 87% male — inference limited to male retail bettors.
  • 92% under 35 — inference limited to younger bettors.
  • Skews working-to-middle class — may not generalize to high-net-worth bettors or the >$500K-volume cohort Citizens JMP identified as the only profitable Kalshi tier.

Pre-registered language

“Findings apply to U.S.-residing active sportsbook bettors with Fade Sync accounts, with minimal prior prediction-market activity. Based on the founding-member survey (n=23), the recruitable population is concentrated geographically (Florida), demographically (87% male, 92% age 21–30), and by sportsbook (96% Hard Rock Bet). The study does not generalize to retail traders broadly, to Kalshi's overall user base, or to high-volume bettors. Findings are interpreted as describing the population Kalshi is actively marketing to and acquiring. Final cohort distributions are reported separately when enrollment completes.”

How the study runs

Each user is their own control.

Fade Sync captures every participant's full sportsbook history as a baseline, then tracks the same person through 8 weeks of Kalshi-exclusive sports trading. No cross-user noise — we compare each bettor against themselves.

01

Recruit & onboard

1,000 active U.S. sportsbook bettors with little to no prediction-market experience join through the Fade app. Onboarding is staggered over week one.

02

Baseline

Fade Sync pulls each user's full sportsbook history — handle, bets, prices, outcomes — from the books they actually use. Min. 90 days, 50 bets.

03

Treatment

8 consecutive weeks trading sports contracts on Kalshi via read-only OAuth. Min. 30 Kalshi trades per user for the primary analysis.

04

Publish

Public whitepaper at week 12. Anonymized data and analysis code released openly on OSF, pre-committed before any user trades.

What we measure

Forecast accuracy, not just P&L.

The headline statistics are strictly proper scoring rules that converge faster than dollar returns — so luck washes out and skill shows through over a realistic window.

Primary outcomes

  • Brier score — per-trade forecast calibration (lower is better)
  • Log loss — per-trade forecast accuracy (lower is better)
  • Closing-line value — entry price vs. vig-removed closing price at the user's own book or Kalshi mid

Secondary & descriptive

  • Net P&L per user — gross and net of fees
  • Maker vs. Taker fill share by user and contract type
  • Implied maker margin on RFQ parlay quotes
  • RFQ refusal rate — requests that produced no fillable quote
Pre-registered hypotheses

Five falsifiable bets,
locked in advance.

Each is tested independently and publishes in either direction, with Bonferroni correction applied across all five at α = 0.01.

H1

Venue effect on CLV

Cohort median Kalshi CLV is no better than their median sportsbook CLV.

H2

Counterparty composition

Institutional market-maker share exceeds Kalshi's stated “7 percent or lower.”

H3

Retail base-rate replication

Cohort Taker return clusters at or below the academic Taker baseline of −32%.

H4

Parlay margin

Cohort parlay implied maker margin exceeds the 15% documented venue average.

H5

Size-dependent pricing

RFQ maker margin scales monotonically with request size — bigger orders, worse prices.
